What is E-Waste?

Electronic waste, or e-waste, refers to discarded electrical or electronic devices. Used electronics which are destined for reuse, resale, salvage, recycling, or disposal are also considered e-waste.

Common e-waste items include computers, televisions, mobile phones, printers, refrigerators, and other household appliances. These items contain valuable materials that can be recovered, as well as hazardous substances that require special handling.

Did you know? A single computer can contain up to 2kg of lead, and flat screen TVs contain mercury - both toxic substances that can leach into soil and water.

Environmental Impact of E-Waste

Hazardous Materials in Electronics

Electronic devices contain numerous toxic substances that can be harmful to human health and the environment when improperly disposed of:

  • Lead (in CRT monitors and soldering)
  • Mercury (in flat-screen displays)
  • Cadmium (in semiconductors and batteries)
  • Brominated flame retardants (in plastic casings)
  • Hexavalent chromium (as corrosion protector)
  • Beryllium (in power supply boxes)

Environmental Pollution

When e-waste is improperly disposed of in landfills, toxic substances can leach into the soil and groundwater, contaminating water supplies and ecosystems.

In many developing countries, e-waste is burned in open pits to recover valuable metals, releasing toxic fumes and particles into the air that can travel long distances and affect large populations.

The informal recycling sector in some countries often uses primitive methods to extract materials, exposing workers and nearby communities to dangerous chemicals.

Wasted Resources

Electronics contain valuable materials that can be recovered and reused, including:

  • Gold, silver, platinum, and palladium (precious metals)
  • Copper, aluminum, and steel (base metals)
  • Rare earth elements (used in magnets and batteries)

Recycling these materials reduces the need for environmentally destructive mining operations and saves energy compared to extracting and processing virgin materials.

The Human Cost

Improper e-waste disposal disproportionately affects developing countries where much of the world's e-waste is shipped. Workers, including children, often dismantle electronics by hand without protective equipment, exposing themselves to toxic substances that can cause serious health problems.

What You Can Do

Recycle Properly

Find certified e-waste recyclers in your area. Look for R2 or e-Stewards certified facilities that follow environmentally sound practices.

Extend Device Life

Keep electronics longer, repair when possible, and upgrade components instead of replacing entire devices.

Donate or Sell

Working electronics can be donated to schools, nonprofits, or sold to others who can use them.

Buy Responsibly

Choose electronics with longer warranties, repairability scores, and from companies with take-back programs.

Battery Care

Remove batteries before disposal and recycle them separately. Use rechargeable batteries when possible.

Spread Awareness

Educate friends and family about e-waste issues and proper disposal methods in your community.

Manufacturer Take-Back Programs

Many electronics manufacturers offer free take-back programs for their products. Check with the manufacturer of your device to see if they have a recycling program. Some retailers also offer trade-in programs or accept old electronics when you purchase new ones.
